How I like to work

Erik Aaserud

This text is adapted from a motivation letter I wrote — but rewritten to not be an application. I’m using it as a clear description of what kind of practice I’m trying to build.

The short version

I want my design practice to be more artistic, intuitive, and exploratory.

I’ve learned a lot from structured, method-heavy design processes — research, argumentation, user studies — and I’m grateful for that. But when the process gets too controlled, I start to lose the part of the work I care about most:

  • the play
  • the curiosity
  • the physical thinking
  • the trust in making as a way of deciding

Material and process first

For me, an intuitive practice means entering a project with openness and allowing the material and the hands to have real influence.

Instead of deciding the final form at the beginning, I try to let form appear through testing, adjusting, repeating, building and rebuilding, and paying attention to what the object “wants”.

I don’t treat this as anti-thinking. It’s just a different kind of thinking: decisions made through the workshop, not only through language.

Form Factory

In Form Factory, I made one candle holder per day, from idea to finished object.

The short time frame forced me to:

  • make fast decisions
  • trust instinct rather than analysis
  • accept that not everything has to be optimized

This way of working felt both playful and concentrated. It produced many different and unexpected results — and confirmed something important for me: I develop best when I’m making.

What I bring to a group

I tend to show up with a calm, focused energy. I enjoy workshop time, work thoroughly with form and material, and I like processes that can develop over time.

I’m also curious about other people’s practices. Conversation and collaboration are part of how I learn.

The thing I’m trying to protect

I want to design in a way that keeps the work alive while it’s being made.
